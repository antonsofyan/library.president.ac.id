**Navigating AI and Academic Integrity**

Universitas Kristen Indonesia, 18-19 Juni 2026 – In an effort to continually adapt to the rapid advancements of technology in higher education, Neilany Edwina, Head of the President University Library Bureau, alongside Laurensius Yoel Fabian, Reference Librarian, attended a comprehensive national seminar and talkshow focused on the pivotal role of libraries in the age of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Literacy 4.0. The event brought together leading academics and information professionals to discuss the ethical integration of Generative AI, academic integrity, and the evolving identity of librarians as essential learning partners.

**The Core Blueprint: Libraries as the 'Memory' of Universities**

The seminar commenced with a foundational presentation by Dr. Hulman Panjaitan, who established the legal and conceptual definitions of librarianship according to the Indonesian Dictionary (KBBI) and national legislation. Dr. Hulman addressed a fundamental question: Do we still need libraries in the digital era?

He firmly asserted that libraries remain indispensable for academic and learning needs. Despite the rapid growth of Google and Artificial Intelligence (AI), both platforms remain inadequate as they lack full reliability and academic accountability.

"A campus without a library is akin to a university without a memory," Dr. Hulman stated.

He further elaborated on the multifaceted functions of a library as a hub for education, research, preservation, information, and recreation that directly supports the national education system and the Tridarma Perguruan Tinggi (Three Pillars of Higher Education). In this ecosystem, librarians serve as crucial allies for lecturers, researchers, and students. They act as information literacy curators and guardians of academic integrity against the threats of plagiarism—including verbatim copying, ideas presented without paraphrasing, and self-plagiarism.

**AI, Ethics, and Formulating Campus Regulations**

The discussion transitioned into a crucial session moderated by Widya Damayanti, M.Sc., featuring Dr. Rijanto Purbojo, which explored "AI, Ethics, and Integrity: The Role of Christian Higher Education Institutions in Formulating Regulations for AI Use in Learning." Grounded in Indonesian national regulations, Dr. Rijanto emphasized that any institutional AI policy must adhere to core principles: human oversight, accountability, transparency, safety, security, privacy, data protection, inclusivity, reliability, and human rights.

To preserve academic integrity, universities must explicitly communicate AI policies, establish clear expectations within syllabi, and foster trusting relationships with students through authentic assessments. Dr. Rijanto highlighted that while the implications of Generative AI (GenAI) carry inherent risks, they can be mitigated through preventative actions, critical thinking development, and ensuring students do not over-rely on technology. Evidence of genuine student work must remain verifiable through robust citations, references, and verbal explanations.

Sharing a case study from Universitas Pelita Harapan (UPH), he suggested that rector-level umbrella regulations should serve as adaptable guidelines for study programs. Within this framework, librarians must step up as essential partners by leveraging GenAI as an assistant tool to upgrade library databases and shifting traditional information literacy toward comprehensive "AI literacy."

**Talkshow: Confronting the Overload of Literacy 4.0**

The seminar culminated in an interactive talkshow moderated by Tintien Koerniawati, M.I.Kom., featuring prominent experts Agus Rifai, Ph.D. and Kalarensi Naibaho, M.Hum.

The speakers deconstructed the concept of Literacy 4.0. Agus Rifai explained that Literacy 4.0 is a revolutionary phase marked by a massive surge in internet interaction, leading to information overload. This contrasts sharply with previous eras (Literacy 1.0 focused on print literacy, 2.0 on comprehension, and 3.0 on digital transition). Kalarensi Naibaho illustrated this with the phenomenon of WhatsApp AI, noting that because users interface with different algorithms, the massive influx of big data and the Internet of Things (IoT) demands constant verification.

Addressing the rising dependency on AI and the decline of data privacy, both speakers agreed that banning AI is futile. Instead, librarians must stand at the forefront of AI ethics. Agus Rifai introduced the concept of librarians as "AI-enabled learning partners" who maintain contextual boundaries, warning that without critical thinking, AI could shift from a tool to a human substitute.

Kalarensi added that while today's students can generate content instantly through simple prompts, the human element—specifically emotion and feeling—remains irreplaceable. To counter academic dishonesty, both lecturers and librarians must fine-tune their "internal alarms" to detect generic AI-generated language and monitor unnatural leaps in a student's writing progress. From a structural standpoint, Agus noted a shifting trend where modern students look for general data rather than traditional journal databases, urging libraries to remain adaptive, active, and impactful to avoid obsolescence.

**Technological Automations and Data Visualization**

The event also showcased practical technological solutions for modern libraries. Nicholas Sio Pradiva, S.IP. demonstrated the fundamentals of Robotic Process Automation (RPA) utilizing UIPath software, showcasing how digital robots can replicate human actions to automate data input into Library Management Systems. Following this, Chandra Pratama Setiawan, M.Sc. presented a guide to creating clean, data-driven library dashboards using Tableau to optimize library reporting and statistics.

To wrap up the series of activities, the event concluded with a specialized workshop on the utilization of the JASP application delivered by Dr. Dhama Gustiar Baskoro, S.S., M.Pd. This workshop was specifically designed to equip librarians with advanced statistical data analysis skills, directly reinforcing their strategic roles as research consultants, co-teachers, and vital partners to faculties and researchers in navigating modern data-driven research.(Laurensius Yoel Fabian)
